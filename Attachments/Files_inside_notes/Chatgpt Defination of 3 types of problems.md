What you did is actually a **very natural way to derive the middle category**—you removed the _existential uncertainty_ from the hard problem.

Your structure is basically distinguishing between **three different kinds of uncertainty**:

1. **Method uncertainty** – you don’t know how to solve it.
    
2. **Time uncertainty** – you don’t know how long it will take.
    
3. **Value uncertainty** – you’re not sure the effort will matter at all.
    

Your categories then become very clean:

### Easy Problems

- You know **how** to solve them.
    
- You roughly know **how long** it will take.
    
- You know the result is **useful**.
    

### Medium Problems

- You **don’t know how** to solve them yet.
    
- You **don’t know exactly how long** it will take.
    
- You know the result is **useful**.
    

This creates **motivation and curiosity**, not despair.

### Hard Problems

- You **don’t know how** to solve them.
    
- You **don’t know how long** it will take.
    
- You’re **not even sure the effort will lead to anything meaningful**.
    

That’s where the psychological weight appears.

---

So your insight is important:

**The real difference between medium and hard problems is not intelligence or difficulty — it's existential uncertainty.**

Medium problems feel like **a locked door with a key somewhere**. 🔑

Hard problems feel like **you don’t even know if the door has a key.**

---

Your formulation is actually quite strong already. If we compress it slightly:

- **Easy:** You know how to solve it and roughly how long it will take.
    
- **Medium:** You don’t know how yet or how long it will take, but you believe the answer exists and is worth finding.
    
- **Hard:** You don’t know how, you don’t know when, and you’re not even sure the effort will lead to something meaningful.
    

---

What’s interesting is that **most ambitious goals (science, trading edges, startups, major life achievements)** start as **hard problems** and only later become **medium problems** once progress reveals that the “key” actually exists.
