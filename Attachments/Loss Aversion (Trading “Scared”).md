
==**Description:**== Loss aversion is an emotional bias where the fear of losing leads to overly conservative decisions. A loss-averse trader might set stops too close (to avoid any loss), bail out of winners too early (protecting tiny profit), or avoid trades unless they are “certain.” They may refuse to enter obvious setups because they fear the stop will hit. This results in missed opportunities or very poor R:R trades.  
**Underlying Trap:** At its core is fear. Psychologically, losses hurt more than equivalent gains feel good[investopedia.com](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/t/trading-psychology.asp#:~:text=,acceptable%20from%20a%20rational%20standpoint). The trader becomes risk-averse to a fault. The SMC trader might be afraid to trade around key levels or may move stops to breakeven at the slightest pullback.  

==**Manifestation:**== A typical manifestation is a parade of small wins and larger losses, and slow account growth. The trader often laments “I should have held” or “I’m leaving money on the table,” yet still sticks to tiny profit targets or escapes trades at the first sign of trouble. For example, after a small win, a loss-averse trader might immediately pocket a few dollars rather than ride the trend, because they dread giving back a single penny. The TIOmarkets guide describes this as “trading scared,” where traders “avoid entering or move stops to break even too quickly”[tiomarkets.com](https://tiomarkets.com/es/article/9-psychological-trading-mistakes-and-how-to-avoid-them#:~:text=5,scared).  



==**How to Overcome:**== The key is acceptance of risk. Use fixed-percentage risk so that any individual loss is small (making it “bearable” psychologically). Gradually acclimate: if a 2% risk feels scary, paper-trade those stops first. Remind yourself that a loss is inevitable and the goal is consistency. Practically, set a firm stop-loss – not based on emotion – and leave it. Use preset profit targets (e.g. 3:1 R:R) so you’re aiming for bigger winners. Also, backtest or review your stats: seeing that a 30% win rate with a 3:1 ratio is profitable may reduce fear. Finally, keep perspective: maintain an account buffer so one loss never wipes you out. As one coach notes, you should “make sure that when you do lose, it won’t matter that much”[tiomarkets.com](https://tiomarkets.com/es/article/9-psychological-trading-mistakes-and-how-to-avoid-them#:~:text=The%20best%20way%20to%20avoid,it%20won%E2%80%99t%20matter%20that%20much). With practice, resisting losses becomes easier, and you’ll capture larger trends rather than just small fluctuations.
